00 - Executive Summary🔗

Executive Summary

This report presents the results of a multi-month cyber threat intelligence investigation into an Egyptian threat actor operating under the aliases Revesky, wa0_3, and Bovax.

The investigation began after we identified a Telegram account advertising access to Egyptian government databases. What initially appeared to be a single seller was gradually uncovered as part of a larger operation targeting government entities, universities, and organizations across Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Iraq, and Kuwait.

Throughout the investigation, we collected and connected evidence from multiple independent sources, including Telegram groups, underground forums, leaked databases, deleted messages, historical usernames, blockchain artifacts, NFT gift transfers, and operational security mistakes. No single finding identified the actor on its own, but together they revealed the structure of the operation, its infrastructure, and the people behind it.

During the investigation, we independently verified that the actor had live access to services associated with Vodafone Egypt, demonstrating that the operation relied on active system access in addition to previously obtained data. We also identified a second administrator, known as "Maro", and successfully linked him to a real-world identity through independent investigative findings, providing further insight into the people involved in the operation.

Based on the evidence presented in this report, we assess with High Confidence that the aliases Bovax and wa0_3 are operated by the same individual. We further assess with Medium Confidence that the alias Revesky is operated by the same individual, based on multiple independent findings that consistently point to the same attribution.

The evidence collected during this investigation has been securely preserved and shared with the appropriate law enforcement authorities to support further investigation and any necessary legal action.

INTRODUCTION & METHODOLOGY🔗

In late 2025, we identified a Telegram account advertising access to Egyptian government databases. That discovery marked the beginning of a multi-month investigation that uncovered an interconnected network of underground forums, private Telegram groups, and threat actor personas operating under the aliases Revesky, WA0_3, and Bovax.

As the investigation progressed, we uncovered a Telegram bot capable of querying data associated with the Egyptian Ministry of Supply and validated that the actor possessed live access to services associated with Vodafone Egypt. The same aliases were subsequently linked to breach advertisements and leaked data involving government entities, universities, and organizations across Egypt, Iraq, Saudi Arabia, and Kuwait, revealing an operation that extended well beyond isolated database leaks.

Rather than relying on a single breakthrough, the attribution was built by correlating dozens of independent findings. Closed Telegram communities, recovered deleted messages, historical forum activity, infrastructure analysis, Telegram NFT gift tracking, blockchain artifacts, and operational security (OpSec) mistakes each contributed a small piece of the puzzle. Individually, none of these artifacts identified the actor. Together, they formed a high-confidence attribution linking multiple online identities to the same operator.

This report reconstructs that investigation from beginning to end, documenting how each lead was discovered, validated, and connected until the complete picture emerged.

How to Read This Report

This report is structured as a chronological investigative story each section builds upon the previous one. We start from the first thread (the Bovax Telegram account) and follow it step by step until we reach the true identity and the final linkage to Revesky. Every claim is supported by visual evidence (screenshots, API responses, recovered deleted messages). We strongly recommend reading the sections in order.

01 - The First Thread: Bovax Appears in Hacker Groups🔗

INITIAL DETECTION ( TELEGRAM GROUPS )

As a CTI firm, we have long maintained a presence in private hacker Telegram groups monitoring discussions, engaging where appropriate, and tracking threat actors over extended periods. During this monitoring, an account operating under the alias Bovax (Telegram ID: 7955791415) drew our attention.

Bovax claimed to have direct access to the systems of the Egyptian Ministry of Supply (msit-cms.com). To establish credibility within these underground communities, he launched a promotional offer, telling members:

"Come on guys, first 20 national IDs i will search for free."

The offer meant that the first 20 users who submitted an Egyptian National ID number would receive a free lookup against what Bovax claimed was the Ministry of Supply's database. By providing these complimentary searches, he attempted to demonstrate that he possessed genuine access to the ministry's records and to build trust among potential buyers.

After using these free lookups to convince members of the authenticity of his claims, Bovax escalated his offering by advertising full access to the ministry's infrastructure, posting messages such as:

"Who wants to buy Egyptian Ministry of Supply access?"

Evidence: Bovax offers Ministry of Supply database searches before advertising server access

Bovax initially built credibility by offering free searches against what he claimed was the Egyptian Ministry of Supply database. After establishing trust with group members, he began advertising the sale of direct access to the ministry's systems.

As we continued investigating Bovax, we used Dexpose's intelligence platform to search across Telegram communities and reconstruct his activity. This allowed us to recover his messages from numerous public hacker groups, as well as several private groups where he had been active.

Among these messages, Bovax repeatedly claimed that he was responsible for breaching nearly half of Iraq's universities, presenting himself as the threat actor behind many of those compromises. He also stated that he had "obtained more than 6 CVEs".

At that stage of the investigation, we had not yet realized that these claims were only the beginning of a much larger operation.

04 - Does He Actually Have the Vodafone System?🔗

LIVE API ACCESS VERIFICATION

This is not an old leak, it's a live access

The hidden Vodafone section in the bot piqued our curiosity. The most important question: Is this just an old leaked database? Or does he actually have live access to Vodafone's internal system? To answer this question, we designed a foolproof test.

Verification Plan: I contacted Bovax pretending to be a serious buyer interested in purchasing direct API access to the Vodafone system. However, before paying, I wanted to verify his credibility. Therefore:

  1. I purchased a brand new Vodafone line registered in my name , a line that did not exist in any previous leak because it was new.
  2. I sent the new line number to Bovax and asked him to query it as a test.
  3. The response came back in seconds , a complete JSON response from the internal Vodafone system.
  4. I compared the results with the original contract , 100% match: name, national ID, governorate, plan type, and account status.

Analysis: How can he access it?

The response structure matches the Vodafone TM Forum Open API. This suggests one of three scenarios: (A) Compromised internal employee credentials, (B) An IDOR vulnerability in an improperly authenticated API endpoint. or (C) A breach of a third-party vendor integrating with Vodafone's system. In all cases, this is direct, live access not a leaked database.
